# Case Study: Should an Algorithm Tell You Who to Promote?

<p style="text-align: center;">By Jeffrey T. Polzer</p>

<p style="text-align: center;">Harvard Business Review — June 2018</p>

(1) Aliyah Jones, a VP for Becker-Birnbaum International (BBI), a global consumer product company, needed a marketing director for her division's 34 products. After working with HR to narrow down the finalists, she had two candidates: Molly Ashworth, a brand manager on her team in the cleaning division, and Ed Yu, a rising star from BBI's beauty division.

(2) Aliyah liked Molly and respected her work. Two years earlier, Molly had spearheaded a new subscription service for BBI cleaning products, which had shown strong growth. Having mentored Molly through the launch of the service, Aliyah was intimately familiar with her protégé's strengths and was certain she was ready for the next challenge.

(3) However, Christine Jenkins, a corporate VP of HR, recommended Ed Yu. As a brand manager in beauty, Ed revived its FreshFace product line, increasing sales by 60% in three years. Like Molly, he had been quickly tapped as a high potential and was recommended as a 96% match for the job by HR's new people-analytics system. Molly had been an 83% match.

## The Interview

(4) During his interview, Ed seemed smart and ambitious, but his answers seemed a bit stiff. He explained how his division's approach to in-field customer research could work in cleaning. Aliyah didn't sense the dynamism or entrepreneurial mindset that she knew Molly had. Molly's interview was next. She demonstrated deep knowledge of the business and had good ideas for collaborating across programs. She was as polished and thoughtful as Ed, but she also seemed warmer and more self-aware. "Knocked it out of the park," Aliyah thought.

## The Algorithm

(5) The day after, Aliyah met with Christine and Brad Bibson, a data scientist. Brad handed over network analyses of Molly's and Ed's e-mail and meeting history at BBI. It was clear that Ed was connected to key people in other groups. Molly's network was mainly within cleaning products.

(6) "You wouldn't launch a new product without data," Christine said. "HR decisions should be approached the same way. We're confident that decisions made using our algorithms are reasoned, strong, and less biased by personal feelings toward employees."

(7) Brad Bibson encouraged healthy skepticism. "Our algorithm is brand-new. We've used it to inform three promotion decisions so far, but it's too early to tell how those people are doing." Aliyah sighed. "I'd trust the algorithm more if I understood its methodology better."

(8) "I have to take a broader view," Christine said. "We ran analysis to show which high potentials are at risk of leaving BBI, and Ed was near the top of the list. There is not likely to be an opening in beauty products, and we want to keep him." Aliyah noted that Molly would be devastated if she didn't get the job. "Our analysis didn't flag her as a flight risk," Brad said.

A week later, Aliyah wasn't any closer to a decision. Should she trust the algorithm or her instincts?

## Expert Response: Prasad Setty (Vice President of People Operations, Google)

(9) Prasad Setty believes that people analytics is best applied as a complement to, not a substitute for, human judgment. In the case of BBI, the people analytics system appears to have done a good job in surfacing Ed, an unexpected candidate. If the algorithm is going to make the promotion decision, the burden of proof is very high. Aliyah is right to want more clarity on the system's methodology.

(10) If success means bringing a talented marketing director on board who can operate at the highest velocity immediately, then Molly appears to be the better fit. If Aliyah is more keen on increasing collaboration with other parts of the company than Ed, who has a wider network of relationships, then Ed seems to be the smarter choice. In either case, it's important for Aliyah to own the decision. The goal now is to arm leaders with data and context, but not to undercut their role in the process. Human judgment remains the final word.
